# Why block printing is having a moment in European fashion — and why most brands are doing it wrong

*Published 14 March 2025 · By Adéla Kovářová, Indo-European Fusion Fashion*

---

Walk into any concept store in Berlin or Vienna right now and you'll find block-printed fabric. It's on tote bags, cushion covers, wrap dresses. It's everywhere. Which is fine — except that in 90% of cases, the print was not made by a woodblock at all. It was digitally simulated and run through a rotary screen printer in Bangladesh. The "handmade" label refers to something that happened at a factory, not a workshop.

This matters for a few reasons, and not just the ethical ones.

A real block-printed textile looks different. The repeat isn't mathematically perfect. The ink density varies slightly across a single motif because the craftsperson applies pressure by hand and the wooden block absorbs pigment differently as it dries. You get what printers in Jaipur call *chhapai ki khaamiyaan* — "the imperfections of printing" — and experienced buyers in Europe now recognise these marks as proof of process, not defects.

Digital simulation gets it wrong in a specific way. The irregularity is randomised by an algorithm. Real block-print irregularity follows the logic of human fatigue, block wear, and fabric grain. These are different kinds of imperfection and a trained eye can tell.

---

## What's actually driving demand

Two things, probably.

First, sustainable fashion writing has spent the last four years explaining supply chains in detail that would have been considered excessive in 2018. A reader who has been through that material knows what Fairtrade certification covers and what it doesn't, knows the difference between organic cotton and recycled polyester, and knows roughly how long a garment should last at a given price point. This reader is harder to fool.

Second, the pandemic interrupted international travel for two years, and when travel came back, spending on experience goods — things that carry a story — went up across most European markets. A block-printed jacket from a named workshop in Bagru is a portable experience. A machine-printed version of the same pattern is not.

---

## The problem with "inspired by" collections

High-street brands have been quick. You can now buy block-print-inspired pieces at Zara, H&M, and their mid-market equivalents in 12 European countries. The price point is CZK 600–900 for a blouse.

The problem isn't that they exist. The problem is what they do to buyer expectations. A customer who buys a 700 CZK "block print" blouse from Zara, wears it twice, and finds it pilling, is now someone who associates block-print aesthetics with low durability. That's a direct market-education problem for brands that are actually producing genuine hand-printed pieces at a higher price.

We price our Bagru-dyed tops at 2 800–3 400 CZK because that's what the material, the artisan's time, and a viable margin actually require. ## What to look for when you buy

If you're buying block-printed clothing anywhere, not just from us, here's what genuine hand-printing looks like:

- **Ink bleed at the edges** — the pigment spreads very slightly into the fabric weave. A crisp, perfectly hard edge is a sign of screen or digital printing.
- **Back-face transfer** — hold the fabric to light. With natural dyes on cotton, you'll see colour on the reverse. Synthetic pigment on a screen print stays on the surface.
- **Repeat variation** — measure two identical motifs in the repeat. They will differ by 1–3mm in a hand-stamped piece. Perfect repeats are machine-made.
- **QR traceability** — some producers now link garments to their origin workshop. We do this. It's a reasonable thing to ask for.

---

Block printing is not a trend. It's a 400-year-old trade that survived industrialisation because it produces things machines can't. The current fashion moment will pass. The prints won't.
