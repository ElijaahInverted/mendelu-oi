## Page 1: Home

**Hero section**

> Small-batch fashion printed by hand in Rajasthan. Designed for how people in Central Europe actually dress.

**Intro paragraph**

Indo-European Fusion Fashion makes clothing at the point where South Asian block printing meets European cut. That means working with a woodblock workshop in Bagru, Jaipur — a family business that has been printing the same patterns since the 1960s — and turning their fabric into pieces that work in Prague, Vienna, and Warsaw. Not costume. Not "global inspiration." Actual clothes, made carefully.

**Feature blocks (3 columns)**

1. *Block-printed by hand* — Every print in our collection is stamped by hand using carved wooden blocks. The slight imperfection in each repeat is proof of process, not a defect.

2. *Modular & multi-style* — Several pieces in the collection wrap, tie, or fold into more than one configuration. We design for people who pack light and need one thing that works in two situations.

3. *QR-verified sourcing* — Every garment carries a QR code that links to the artisan workshop that made the fabric. Names, location, process.

## Page 7: Collections — Monsoon Minimalism SS2025

**Page title:** Monsoon Minimalism — Spring/Summer 2025

**Collection text:**

The SS2025 collection is called Monsoon Minimalism because that's the actual design brief we gave ourselves: pieces that make sense in July heat and September layering, using monsoon-season indigo and terracotta — the two colours that define the Bagru dye season.

The monsoon in Rajasthan runs from July to September. During this period, natural indigo dyeing is paused in most workshops because high humidity interferes with the oxidation process. Bagru printers use this time to prepare fabrics, repair woodblocks, and carve new patterns. The collection was developed during those months from fabrics prepared the previous year.

Six pieces. Two jackets, one blouse, one pair of trousers, one skirt, one scarf. Each one pairs with at least two others in the collection.

## Page 9: Craft & Process — How It's Made

**Page title:** How It's Made

Block printing works like this: a craftsperson carves a pattern into a block of teak or sheesham wood, typically 15–20 cm across. The block is pressed into a tray of natural pigment — indigo paste, iron-oxide terracotta, or black from tamarind seeds — and then stamped onto fabric stretched across a padded printing table. One stamp, one motif. The block is re-inked and stamped again, advancing across the fabric by hand.

A skilled printer at the Bagru workshop we work with — Shyam Sunder Chhipa — can print 8–10 metres of fabric per hour on simple patterns. The geometric lattice on TAN-JKT-001 is not a simple pattern. It takes closer to 4 metres per hour because the repeat requires precise alignment from four different block angles.

**Step by step:**

1. **Fabric preparation** — Cotton is boiled in a solution of *harda* (myrobalan) to prepare the cellulose fibres to absorb natural dye. This step takes 24 hours.
2. **Block preparation** — Wooden blocks are soaked overnight to prevent them splitting under pressure. New blocks are carved to order; existing blocks in the workshop date back 30–40 years.
3. **Printing** — Done in natural light, morning hours. The workshop in Bagru has a long open-sided printing shed facing east.
4. **Drying** — Printed fabric is hung on bamboo poles in the courtyard. In Bagru's dry climate, drying takes 2–4 hours in summer.
5. **Fixing** — Depending on the dye, fabric is either sun-fixed (indigo) or washed in an alum solution (iron-oxide pigments).
6. **Washing** — Final wash in clean water to remove unfixed pigment. The water from this process returns colour to the Bandikui river system — a known environmental issue the workshop is working to address through a filtration system installed in 2022.
7. **Quality check** — Each metre is inspected by hand. We reject fabric where the print misregistration exceeds 5mm or where dye penetration is inconsistent.

## Page 10: Craft & Process — Meet the Artisans

**Page title:** Meet the Artisans

**Shyam Sunder Chhipa — Lead Printer, Bagru**

Shyam has been printing at his family's workshop since he was 14. He's now 41. His father taught him the craft; his father's father before that. The Chhipa community in Bagru has been associated with block printing for several hundred years — *chhipa* itself is a Hindi word derived from *chhapna*, to print.

He works six days a week, roughly six hours of printing per day. The rest of the time goes to block maintenance, fabric preparation, and training two apprentices from the neighbouring village.

Indo-European Fusion Fashion pays 35% above the local market rate for printed fabric because Shyam's quality control is better than the other workshops we visited in Bagru and Sanganer. This is a business decision, not a charity decision.

**Priya Sharma — Quality Control, Jaipur**

Priya joined the operation in 2023. She worked previously for a garment exporter in Jaipur for six years, inspecting finished goods for European retail buyers. She knows what European customers expect from fabric. She's the person who rejected three pieces from the AW2024 run because the kantha stitching tension was uneven — a rejection we disagreed with at the time and turned out to be right about.

She inspects every piece before it ships from Jaipur.

## Page 14: Our Story

**Page title:** Our Story

Indo-European Fusion Fashion started in 2023 with one question: why is it so hard to find clothing that takes South Asian craft seriously without turning it into a costume?

The founders — two students at Mendel University in Brno with a shared background in design and economics — spent a semester studying Indo-European trade history and came out of it convinced that the interesting territory was not in "fusion food" or "fusion music" but in fabric. Specifically in the gap between what Rajasthani block printers actually produce and what ends up on the racks of European stores as "ethnic-inspired."

The first sourcing trip to Jaipur happened in January 2024. We visited eleven workshops in Bagru and Sanganer over four days. We worked with two. By April we had a debut collection of three pieces.

We're not a large company. We have no warehouse. We work with a logistics partner in Prague who handles fulfilment from a temperature-controlled storage unit in Brno. We don't have a physical store and we're not planning one in the near term.

What we do have is a direct relationship with the workshop that makes our fabric, a clear idea of what the pieces should do, and a preference for being specific rather than vague.
