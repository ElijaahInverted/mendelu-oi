# One jacket, five occasions: how modular dressing actually works

*Published 2 April 2025 · By Tereza Blahová, TĀNTU*

---

I've been wearing our Indigo Wrap Jacket for three months now. I bought it with the intention of testing it properly before writing about it, which means I've worn it on a train to Vienna, at a client presentation in Prague, at a friend's vernissage in Brno, to the farmers' market on a cold Saturday morning, and to dinner at a restaurant where the lighting was low enough that I felt slightly underdressed in it, then immediately didn't.

That's five occasions. One jacket. Here's how each one actually worked.

---

## 1. The train to Vienna (travel, 6 hours)

Long-distance trains in Central Europe are unpredictable in temperature. The ÖBB Railjet is aggressively air-conditioned in summer and erratic in winter. I wore the wrap jacket over a cotton long-sleeve and carried nothing heavier.

The key feature for travel: the wrap style means you can loosen or tighten it without unbuttoning anything. When the train got cold, I pulled the wrap tighter and tucked the overlap. When it got stuffy near Mikulov, I loosened it to just a collar. It doubles as a light blanket if you fold it across your lap, which sounds stupid but I did it and it worked.

No pockets is the only real complaint. I had to keep my ticket in my hand.

---

## 2. The client presentation (professional, Prague)

I wore it over a plain white Uniqlo shirt and dark straight trousers. If you photograph this combination it looks deliberately considered. In person it reads as dressed-up without looking like you tried. The indigo print is geometric enough to be legible in a meeting room and unusual enough that three people asked where I got it, which was useful.

The structure of the jacket holds at the shoulders without a stiff inner lining. It drapes, but it doesn't collapse. For a presentation where you're standing and moving around, that matters.

---

## 3. The vernissage (evening, Brno gallery)

A vernissage crowd in Brno is mostly artists, architects, and the friends of both. The dress code is "whatever, but make it interesting." I wore the jacket open over a thin black turtleneck and tapered black trousers. The print did the heavy lifting.

Terracotta-dyed lining against gallery white walls is a good combination. I have photos. It looked better than I expected.

---

## 4. Saturday morning market (casual, outdoor)

This is where it should have failed. Wrap jackets at outdoor markets look strange unless you commit fully to the aesthetic, which I didn't — I had jeans on. But the cotton weight is light enough that it doesn't read as formal in daylight. It looked like a shirt with more structure. A vendor at the cheese stall asked if I was cold and I said no and he said he liked the colour and we had a conversation about natural dyes for six minutes, which I hadn't planned.

---

## 5. Dinner (evening, dimly lit restaurant)

This is the trickiest one. The restaurant had white tablecloths and a wine list with a back page. I felt for a moment that the wrap style was too casual — too bohemian for the setting. Then I looked at the table next to ours and a woman was wearing a silk blouse with a pattern that cost three times as much and read as zero interesting. The block print does something that plain fabric doesn't: it holds attention without demanding it.

Verdict: it worked. I'd wear it there again with the wrap closed and something small at the waist to give it shape.

---

## The actual point

Modular or multistyle clothing is a real category, not a marketing phrase, but only when the garment has genuine structural flexibility — fabric that drapes differently depending on how you hold it, a fastening system that adjusts across a range, a print that reads differently against different backgrounds.

The Indigo Wrap Jacket does those things. Not every TĀNTU piece does — some are just well-made regular garments, and that's fine too. But the wrap pieces in particular are worth trying if you travel regularly and pack light or if you genuinely want one thing that covers more than one situation.

Three months in, I haven't found the occasion where it doesn't work. I'm still looking.
